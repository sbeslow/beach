As a civic hacker, open government advocate, and beach lover, I wanted Drek Beach to use data to give an account of fecal contaimination at Chicago beaches. This site is very much a work in progress. It is also open to contributions for those interested. Join into our project on GitHub

Drek Beach sets out to answer these questions:

  • Which Chicago beaches are closed the most? Why?
  • When a swim advisory or ban is issued, how often is it correct?
  • What can residents do about this?

The Chicago Park District Beaches website is a terrific resource for getting information about visiting the beach today. It also includes current swim advisories, the e-coli level prediction for now, and the last e-coli measurement -- which, due to its 18-hour incubation period -- is from yesterday.

Unfortunately, the latter is not particularly helpful in this context, and there is no way to see prior days. So, on May 22, when beach season began, Drek Beach started monitoring this information and recording a historical record for this season so far.

Ecoli

E-coli is a bacteria found in the intestines of animals. The city monitors the amount of E-coli in the water as an indicator for the amount of fecal contamination. The US E.P.A recommends notifying the public when E. coli bacteria levels are above 235 CCE (Enterococci Calibrator Cell Equivalents per 100 milliliters of water) federal water quality Beach Action Value (BAV), which is 235. If it exceeds this level, there will be a Swim Advisory. When the level exceeds 1000 CCE, the Park District issues a Swim Ban.


The primary source of E-coli at Chicago beaches are animals, sewers, and garbage
